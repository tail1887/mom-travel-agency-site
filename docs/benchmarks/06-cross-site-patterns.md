# 06. Cross-Site Benchmark Patterns

## Purpose

Summarize reusable design, content, and operation patterns from the benchmarked travel agency sites.

## Common Strategic Patterns

### 1. Specialty Positioning Beats Generic Travel

The benchmark sites tend to emphasize a specialty:

- Trekking and cultural exploration
- South America, Africa, and special-region travel
- Elegant adult/senior long-haul travel
- India and special regions
- Small-group backpacking and pilgrimage

Implication:

- The new site should pick a memorable specialty or customer promise before visual design begins.

### 2. Consultation Is A Core Conversion Path

Phone, KakaoTalk, quote inquiry, and explanation sessions appear repeatedly.

Implication:

- Product pages should not end at "view details." They should repeatedly invite phone or Kakao consultation.

### 3. Product Details Need Operational Specificity

Useful product fields include:

- Destination
- Duration
- Departure dates
- Status
- Price
- Deposit
- Minimum departure count
- Tags
- Difficulty or intensity
- Accommodation
- Included/excluded items
- Product manager or consultation contact

Implication:

- The product model should be structured, not just rich text.

### 4. Trust Is Built Through Real Business Information

Important trust signals include:

- Representative phone number
- Business registration information
- Tourism business number
- Insurance or guarantee information
- Address
- Office hours
- Terms
- Privacy policy
- Official account information when relevant

Implication:

- The prototype should include a trust/footer area from the beginning.

### 5. Mature Sites Have More Features Than MVP Needs

Search, login, membership, my page, reservation status, Q&A, and customer center features appear in mature references.

Implication:

- These should be considered P1/P2 unless they directly support the first launch.

## Design Patterns To Adopt

- Clear top navigation with agency, products, customer center, and inquiry paths.
- Destination/category browsing.
- Product cards with status, date, duration, price, and tags.
- Product detail pages with fact summaries before long itinerary text.
- Repeated phone/Kakao consultation CTA.
- Trust-rich footer.
- Mobile-first button and text sizing.

## Design Patterns To Modernize

- Older dense layouts should be simplified for mobile.
- Large product menus should become searchable or card-based.
- Legal/trust information should be readable, not buried.
- Inquiry forms should be short and optional for MVP.

## Patterns To Avoid In MVP

- Full membership system
- Payment automation
- Complex booking inventory
- Too many customer center boards
- Large admin surface before product management is stable

## Prototype Concept Guidance

### Traditional Trust

Use:

- Trust footer
- Customer center
- Large phone/Kakao CTA
- Clear product categories

Avoid:

- Heavy decorative effects
- Small text
- Too many simultaneous promotions

### Premium Custom

Use:

- Destination-led imagery
- Specialty promise
- Curated product highlights
- Consultation-first language

Avoid:

- Price-only competition
- Generic luxury styling with no operational detail

### Product Marketplace

Use:

- Dense but readable product cards
- Date/status/price comparison
- Categories and filters

Avoid:

- Requiring login before inquiry
- Overloading mobile cards

### Consultation Landing

Use:

- Immediate phone/Kakao CTA
- Short trust proof
- Fewer product choices
- Clear office hours and response expectations

Avoid:

- Hiding products completely
- Making the page feel like an ad with no agency substance

### Content/Story

Use:

- Travel stories
- Reviews
- Tour leader expertise
- Related product links

Avoid:

- Blog-heavy operation if the operator cannot maintain content

## Prototype Acceptance Questions

- Can a customer understand the agency specialty within 5 seconds?
- Can a customer find phone or Kakao consultation immediately?
- Can a customer compare product date, duration, status, and price?
- Does the product detail page explain enough to trust the agency?
- Does the design feel modern without losing travel-agency familiarity?
- Can a non-developer operator realistically maintain the visible content?
